Geomorphology: The Shaping of our Islands

🕳️

Karstification

The Puerto Princesa Underground River was carved by Carbonic Acid H2CO3. As rain picks up CO2, it dissolves limestone, creating massive subterranean cathedrals over millions of years.

⛰️

Uplift & Erosion

The Chocolate Hills were once ancient coral reefs. Tectonic shifts lifted them from the sea, and consistent tropical weathering shaped them into the symmetrical "haycock" hills seen today.

🌋

Orogeny

The Philippines sits on the Pacific Ring of Fire. This tectonic activity creates our "Geomorphological Shields"—mountain ranges like the Sierra Madre that physically break apart incoming typhoons.

The Chemistry of the Underground

The process begins when rainwater H2O mixes with atmospheric carbon dioxide CO2, creating a weak solution of Carbonic Acid H2OCO3.

In the Palawan subterranean river, this acid reacts with the limestone CaCOO3 to create calcium bicarbonate, which is soluble in water. Over millions of years, this "melting" of rock creates the massive stalactites and stalagmites we see today.

Uplift & The "Haycock" Mystery

The **Chocolate Hills** of Bohol are a scientific marvel. Two million years ago, this entire region was a vast coral reef under a shallow sea.

Through a process called Tectonic Uplift, the seabed was pushed upward by the movement of the Philippine Mobile Belt. Once exposed to the elements, thousands of years of heavy tropical rainfall eroded the softer limestone, leaving behind over 1,200 symmetrical hills.

Scientific Fact: During the dry season, the green grass turns brown, giving them their famous "Chocolate" appearance.

Orogeny: The Mountain Builders

The Philippines is a "Mobile Belt" trapped between the Eurasian and Philippine Sea plates. Orogeny is the scientific term for the structural deformation of the Earth's lithosphere that creates mountains.

As these plates collide (Subduction), they force the earth upward, creating our "Geomorphological Shields." The Sierra Madre range is a prime example—it stands as a 500km long wall that protects the plains of Luzon from the full force of Pacific typhoons.

Without this specific geomorphology, the climate and safety of the northern islands would be drastically different.
